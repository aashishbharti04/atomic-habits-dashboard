          The Habit Loop

          Every habit runs through the same four-step loop. Understand the loop and you can redesign any habit.

          1Cue

          The trigger that initiates the behavior. It predicts a reward.

          →
          2Craving

          The motivational force. You crave the change in state, not the habit itself.

          →
          3Response

          The actual habit you perform — a thought or an action.

          →
          4Reward

          The end goal. Rewards satisfy the craving and teach the brain to repeat the loop.

          Habits Cheat Sheet — The Four Laws

          1st Law · Make It Obvious

          • Fill out the Habits Scorecard to become aware of your habits.
          • Use implementation intentions: "I will [behavior] at [time] in [location]."
          • Use habit stacking: "After [current habit], I will [new habit]."
          • Design your environment — make the cues of good habits visible.

          Inversion — Make It Invisible: reduce exposure; remove the cues of bad habits from your environment.

          2nd Law · Make It Attractive

          • Use temptation bundling — pair an action you want with one you need.
          • Join a culture where your desired behavior is the normal behavior.
          • Create a motivation ritual — do something you enjoy right before a difficult habit.

          Inversion — Make It Unattractive: reframe your mindset; highlight the benefits of avoiding the bad habit.

          3rd Law · Make It Easy

          • Reduce friction — decrease the steps between you and good habits.
          • Prime the environment — prepare it so future actions are easy.
          • Master the decisive moment — optimize the small choices with big impact.
          • Use the Two-Minute Rule — downscale habits until they take two minutes.
          • Automate — invest in technology and one-time choices that lock in behavior.

          Inversion — Make It Difficult: increase friction; use commitment devices to restrict future choices.

          4th Law · Make It Satisfying

          • Use reinforcement — give yourself an immediate reward on completion.
          • Make "doing nothing" enjoyable when avoiding a bad habit.
          • Use a habit tracker — keep the streak alive and "don't break the chain."
          • Never miss twice — when you forget, get back on track immediately.

          Inversion — Make It Unsatisfying: get an accountability partner; sign a habit contract so breaking the habit is public and painful.

          Questions & Answers

          How long does it take to build a habit?

          The honest answer: forever — because a habit stops being a habit once you stop doing it. What matters isn't time but frequency: habits form based on the number of repetitions, not the number of days. Stop asking "how long" and just put in your reps.

          What should I do when I miss a day?

          Follow the rule: never miss twice. Missing once is an accident; missing twice is the start of a new (bad) habit. The first mistake is never the one that ruins you — it's the spiral of repeated mistakes that follows.

          Where do I start if I want to change?

          Start with identity, not outcomes. Instead of "I want to run a marathon" (outcome), aim for "I am a runner" (identity). Every action you take is a vote for the type of person you wish to become. Small habits win because each one proves your new identity to yourself.

          How do I break a bad habit?

          Invert the four laws: make it invisible (remove the cue), unattractive (reframe the benefits of avoiding it), difficult (increase friction, use commitment devices), and unsatisfying (accountability partner + habit contract).

          Why do small habits matter so much?

          Habits are the compound interest of self-improvement. Getting 1% better every day makes you ~37× better after one year. Success is the product of daily habits, not once-in-a-lifetime transformations.

          What if a habit feels boring or I lose motivation?

          Everyone faces the same challenge: the plateau of latent potential. Habits often appear to make no difference until you cross a critical threshold. The work is never wasted — it's being stored. Professionals stick to the schedule; amateurs let life get in the way.
